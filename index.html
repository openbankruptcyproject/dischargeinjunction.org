Discharge Injunction

A creditor is collecting on a debt that was discharged in bankruptcy. Here is how to stop them, document the violation, file a contempt motion, and recover damages.

If a Creditor Is Violating Your Discharge Right Now

1. Stop communicating verbally. Switch to written communication only -- letters, emails, or certified mail. This creates a paper trail.

2. Send written notice with your bankruptcy case number, discharge date, and a copy of your discharge order. Keep proof of delivery.

3. Document everything. Save every letter, email, voicemail, and text. Screenshot credit reports. Record dates, times, and names.

4. File a motion for contempt if the violation continues after notice. See the step-by-step process below.

What Counts as a Discharge Injunction Violation?

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 524(a)(2), any act to collect, recover, or offset a discharged debt as a personal liability of the debtor violates the discharge injunction. For a general overview of what the discharge injunction is, see discharge injunction protection.

Common Violations

  • Collection calls or letters -- any demand for payment on a discharged debt, whether by the original creditor, a collection agency, or a debt buyer
  • Negative credit reporting -- reporting a discharged debt as "delinquent," "charged off," or "past due" instead of "discharged in bankruptcy" or "included in bankruptcy"
  • Filing a lawsuit -- suing the debtor on a debt that was discharged
  • Wage garnishment -- continuing or initiating garnishment on a discharged debt
  • Repossession attempts -- attempting to repossess property to satisfy a discharged personal liability (note: in rem claims against collateral may survive discharge in some circumstances)
  • Threatening legal action -- verbal or written threats of lawsuits, garnishment, or liens on discharged debts
  • Offsetting bank accounts -- a bank applying the debtor's deposited funds against a discharged debt the bank holds
  • Refusing to release liens -- failing to release a lien that no longer has any legal basis after discharge (though some liens survive discharge as in rem claims)

Important distinction: The discharge injunction prohibits personal liability collection. Some liens survive discharge as in rem claims (claims against the property itself, not the debtor personally). A creditor with a valid lien can enforce it against the collateral even after discharge -- they just cannot pursue the debtor personally for any deficiency. This distinction matters for secured debts like mortgages and car loans.

How to Document a Violation

Strong documentation is the foundation of a successful contempt motion. Courts require clear evidence that (1) a discharge order was entered, (2) the creditor knew about it, and (3) the creditor acted to collect anyway.

Evidence to Collect

  • Discharge order -- get a copy from PACER or your bankruptcy court clerk. This proves the injunction exists.
  • Proof of notice -- the bankruptcy court notifies all scheduled creditors of the discharge. The claims register or mailing matrix proves the creditor was on notice.
  • Collection letters and emails -- keep originals. Note the date received and how it was delivered.
  • Call logs -- record dates, times, phone numbers, duration, and a summary of what was said. In one-party consent states, you may record the calls.
  • Credit reports -- pull reports from all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) through AnnualCreditReport.com. Screenshot the specific entries showing discharged debts reported inaccurately.
  • Bank statements -- if a bank offset your account, the statement shows the deduction.
  • Your written notice to the creditor -- keep a copy of any letter you sent informing them of the discharge, along with proof of mailing (certified mail receipt).

Create a Timeline

Organize your evidence chronologically: when was the debt discharged, when did you first notify the creditor, when did the violation(s) occur, and what was the creditor's response. A clear timeline makes your contempt motion much stronger.

Filing a Motion for Contempt: Step by Step

The discharge injunction is enforced through the bankruptcy court's contempt power. You file a motion in the same court that entered your discharge order.

  1. Obtain your discharge order from PACER or the court clerk. Confirm the debt in question was included in your bankruptcy schedules and was discharged.
  2. Check local rules. Many bankruptcy courts have specific local rules or forms for contempt motions. Check your court's website or call the clerk's office.
  3. Draft the motion. Include: your case number, the date of discharge, identification of the discharged debt, a description of each violation with dates, the creditor's name and address, and your requested relief (see damages section below).
  4. Attach evidence as exhibits: discharge order, collection letters, credit reports, call logs, your written notice to the creditor, and proof of mailing.
  5. File the motion with the bankruptcy court clerk. There may be a filing fee for the motion (typically $0-31 depending on the court and whether it opens a miscellaneous proceeding).
  6. Serve the creditor with a copy of the motion and all exhibits according to the applicable rules of service.
  7. Attend the hearing. The court will schedule a hearing where you present your evidence and the creditor has the opportunity to respond.

Pro se filers: You can file a contempt motion without an attorney. Many bankruptcy court clerks will help you with procedural questions (though they cannot give legal advice). The motion does not need to be perfect -- it needs to clearly communicate what happened. Courts are generally accommodating of pro se filings.

Damages Available for Violations

Courts have broad discretion in fashioning remedies for discharge injunction violations. Available damages include:

Type of Damage Description Typical Range
Actual damages Out-of-pocket losses caused by the violation (bank fees, lost wages, etc.) Varies
Emotional distress Anxiety, stress, and suffering caused by the violation $1,000 -- $25,000
Attorney fees Cost of hiring an attorney to bring the contempt motion Varies
Punitive damages Additional damages for egregious or repeated violations $5,000 -- $100,000+
Sanctions Court-imposed penalties to deter future violations Court's discretion

Circuit split on private right of action: Courts disagree on whether Section 524 creates a private cause of action for damages or whether the only remedy is contempt. In some circuits, damages are available only through the court's contempt power (which requires a showing of willfulness). In others, debtors can bring independent actions. Check the law in your circuit.

The "Willfulness" Standard

Most courts require the violation to be "willful" before awarding damages for contempt. But "willful" does not mean the creditor intended to violate the discharge -- it means the creditor intentionally performed the act that constituted the violation.

In other words, if a creditor deliberately sends a collection letter on a debt it knows was included in a bankruptcy, the violation is willful -- even if the creditor did not realize the letter violated the discharge injunction. The relevant question is whether the creditor intended to send the letter, not whether it intended to break the law.

This is an important distinction. A creditor cannot avoid liability by claiming it did not know the debt was discharged if it was properly scheduled and the court sent notice of the discharge.

Credit Report Violations

Inaccurate credit reporting of discharged debts is one of the most common and most damaging violations. After discharge, creditors should update their reporting to show the debt as "included in bankruptcy" or "discharged in bankruptcy" with a zero balance.

How to Dispute Credit Report Errors

  1. Pull your credit reports from all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com
  2. Identify any discharged debts reported as active, delinquent, or with a balance
  3. File disputes with each credit bureau (online, by mail, or by phone) -- include a copy of your discharge order
  4. File a dispute directly with the creditor/furnisher
  5. If the error is not corrected within 30 days, you may have claims under both the discharge injunction and the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

Credit reporting violations can also support claims under the FDCPA (Fair Debt Collection Practices Act) if the creditor is a "debt collector" as defined by that statute, and under the FCRA for failure to report accurate information after a dispute.

Debt Buyers and the Discharge Injunction

Debts are frequently sold to third-party debt buyers, sometimes multiple times. When a discharged debt is sold, the buyer often does not know (or does not check) whether the debt was discharged. This leads to violations.

Debt buyers are bound by the discharge injunction just like original creditors. Purchasing a discharged debt does not revive the debtor's personal liability. If a debt buyer contacts you about a discharged debt:

  • Send written notice with your case number and discharge date
  • Request validation of the debt under the FDCPA (if the buyer is a debt collector)
  • If collection continues, file a contempt motion and consider FDCPA claims
